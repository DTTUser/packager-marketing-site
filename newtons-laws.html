Newton's Three Laws
of Motion

Three rules that explain almost everything that moves -- from a football to a spacecraft.

Law 1 — Inertia

Objects keep doing what they're doing.

"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at the same speed and in the same direction, unless acted on by an unbalanced force."

In plain terms: things are lazy. A football sitting on the pitch won't move until you kick it. And once it's moving, it'd carry on forever -- if friction and air resistance didn't slow it down.

That resistance to change is called inertia. Heavier objects have more of it -- which is why it's harder to push a car than a trolley.

Law 2 — Force, Mass & Acceleration

More force = more acceleration. More mass = less.

"The acceleration of an object depends on the net force acting on it and its mass: F = ma."

The harder you push something, the faster it accelerates. But heavier objects accelerate less for the same push.

Written as a formula: F = m × a  (Force = mass × acceleration). Rearranged: a = F ÷ m -- so doubling the mass halves the acceleration if the force stays the same.

Law 3 — Action & Reaction

Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

"For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction."

Forces always come in pairs. When you push on something, it pushes back on you with the same force -- just in the opposite direction.

Jump off a skateboard and the board shoots backwards. A rocket burns fuel downwards, and the rocket shoots upwards. You push the ground down when you walk; the ground pushes you forward.
