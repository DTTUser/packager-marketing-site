The
Briefing

You're a senior manager at a mid-sized retailer. A report has landed on your desk. A decision is needed by end of day. How carefully you read what's in front of you will determine what happens next.

Your situation

Sales have been flat for two quarters. Your regional director is pushing for answers. A market research firm has just delivered a report on customer behaviour, and your team is waiting for your recommendation on next steps.

There are four decision points. Each choice affects your critical thinking score. At the end, you'll see what biases shaped your decisions -- and why they matter.

Decision 1 of 4

📊
Customer Sentiment Report Q3 Source not verified
Prepared by: Apex Market Research • Commissioned by: Sales Director

Our survey of 312 customers found that 74% are dissatisfied with in-store experience. Key complaints centred on staff availability and checkout wait times. Competitor NPS scores are running 18 points higher than your current rating.

Note: Survey conducted online over 72 hours. Respondents self-selected via email invitation sent to customers who contacted support in the past 30 days.

Your decision

Your director wants a one-line summary of what this report says. What do you send?

▲ Consequence

Your director forwards your summary to the board. A £200k store refurbishment programme is approved based on a survey of 312 self-selected complainants. Three months later, wider customer data shows overall satisfaction is actually up 6% year on year.

Bias identified: Availability bias + confirmation bias. The most prominent number (74%) was taken at face value. The methodology note -- that respondents were drawn exclusively from recent support contacts -- was overlooked. Dissatisfied customers were always going to dominate this sample.

✓ Good call

Your director appreciates the caveat. Before any budget is committed, the team commissions a broader survey of 2,000 randomly selected customers. The picture is more nuanced -- and the response is proportionate.

Critical thinking in action. You spotted that the sample was self-selecting -- customers invited after a support interaction are not representative of the wider base. One sentence in the methodology note changed the entire framing.

△ Partial credit

Sensible instinct. However, asking for "broader data" without specifying what's wrong with this data means the request gets deprioritised. The director moves forward with a watered-down version of the original plan.

Close -- but vague. You sensed something was off but couldn't name it precisely. Identifying the specific flaw (self-selected complainant sample) would have given your pushback much more weight.

Decision 2 of 4

📰
Industry news Unverified claim
RetailWeekly Newsletter • This morning

Your colleague has forwarded a newsletter with a highlighted section:

"Industry insiders report that retailers investing in self-checkout technology saw an average 23% uplift in customer satisfaction scores last year."

Your colleague has added a note: "This is exactly what we need. Should we fast-track the self-checkout rollout?"

Your decision

How do you respond to your colleague?

▲ Consequence

The statistic appears in your board proposal. During the presentation, a non-executive director asks for the original source. No one can produce it. The proposal is sent back. The delay costs six weeks and significant credibility.

Bias identified: Bandwagon effect + authority bias. "Industry insiders" carries an implied authority that the claim doesn't support. The statistic confirmed what you already wanted to do -- so it wasn't scrutinised.

✓ Good call

Your colleague tracks down the original research. It turns out the 23% figure applies only to large-format grocery retailers -- a completely different context. The team finds more relevant benchmarks instead.

Source scrutiny saves the day. Asking three simple questions -- who, what baseline, what method -- prevented a misleading statistic from reaching the board.

△ Partial credit

Reasonable caution, but the statistic stays in the background conversation and resurfaces later in a slightly different form. Without explicitly questioning the source, you haven't fully neutralised it.

Good instinct, incomplete follow-through. Scepticism without a specific challenge leaves bad data in circulation.

Decision 3 of 4

🎙️
Consultant debrief Conflict of interest
Meeting notes • Retail Experience Consulting Ltd

You've sat in on a 30-minute presentation from an external consultant brought in by the Sales Director. Key takeaway from their slides:

"In our experience working with over 200 retailers, the single highest-impact intervention is always a frontline staff retraining programme. Retailers who invest here see results within 90 days."

The consultant's firm offers a proprietary 12-week retraining programme. Pricing starts at £85,000.

Your decision

The Sales Director asks for your view on whether to proceed with the consultant's programme.

▲ Consequence

The programme is approved. Post-engagement, the "200 retailers" turns out to refer to a mix of advisory sessions, workshops, and full programmes across 15 years. Comparable outcome data for your sector doesn't exist. Results after 90 days are inconclusive.

Bias identified: Authority bias + vague social proof. "200 retailers" sounds like robust evidence but is an unverified aggregate. The consultant's financial interest in recommending their own programme was not factored in.

✓ Good call

The consultant provides three references. One responds. Their experience was positive but in a different sector. You identify a more targeted provider with independently audited results in your specific retail category.

Conflict of interest, caught. Anyone selling a solution has an inherent interest in the problem being confirmed. That doesn't make them wrong -- but it means their evidence needs independent verification.

△ Partial credit

A pilot is agreed. It's a proportionate financial risk, but the underlying question of whether this is the right intervention -- and the right provider -- hasn't been properly tested.

Pragmatic, but incomplete. Managing cost risk is sensible. But a smaller version of the wrong solution is still the wrong solution.

Decision 4 of 4

👥
Team meeting Group consensus pressure
Internal • Your team of six

You've called a team meeting to review the options. Five of your six team members quickly align around one conclusion: the problem is clearly the checkout experience, and the fix is self-checkout. The sixth -- your most junior team member -- quietly suggests the data might point to a staffing levels issue rather than technology.

The room moves on. The junior team member doesn't push back further.

Your decision

How do you handle the meeting?

▲ Consequence

Self-checkout is rolled out. Eighteen months later, an internal review finds that the primary driver of dissatisfaction was staff-to-customer ratios during peak hours -- something self-checkout made worse, not better. The junior team member's read had been correct.

Bias identified: Groupthink + HiPPO effect. Five people agreeing quickly is not the same as five people being right. Junior voices are often suppressed by seniority and social pressure -- and frequently hold the most useful outside perspective.

✓ Good call

The junior team member presents a clear analysis showing peak-hour staffing gaps correlating directly with complaint spikes. The group reconsiders. The eventual recommendation is a staffing model change -- lower cost, faster to implement, and measurably effective within 60 days.

The room nearly missed it. Creating space for the dissenting voice changed the outcome entirely. Good critical thinking in groups means actively protecting minority views from social pressure.

△ Partial credit

The follow-up session happens, but the group dynamic has already set. The majority view carries the day, with minor modifications. The junior team member's core insight doesn't get the hearing it deserved.

Process without intent. Requesting data from both sides is fair, but if the room has already decided, the process becomes theatre. The moment to intervene was in the meeting itself.

Biases in play today

Availability bias -- the most prominent number feels like the most important one. It rarely is.
Confirmation bias -- evidence that fits what we already believe gets waved through unchecked.
Authority bias -- credentials and confident language are not the same as reliable evidence.
Groupthink -- speed of consensus is not a measure of quality of thinking.
